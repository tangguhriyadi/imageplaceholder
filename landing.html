Chapter 1

Why You Should Read This Book

From a business major who couldn't tell you what an API was, to working as an AI Automation Engineer. In under a year.

Let me be honest with you from the start.

I'm not a computer science graduate. I never went to a coding bootcamp. I'm not one of those people who's been programming since they were twelve. In high school, I took the social studies track, the one for kids who supposedly weren't "math people." In college, I majored in business. And before 2022, if you had asked me what an API was, I couldn't have told you.

Today, I work as an AI Automation Engineer for a company overseas. Full-time, fully remote, hired over candidates from all over the world. No CS degree. No bootcamp. No connections in tech. Just a laptop, an internet connection, and a skill I taught myself.

And here's the part that should get your attention: I did all of this from a small city in Indonesia, learning everything in my second language, with no tech industry around me and no one I could ask for help.

If you're reading this from the US, you have faster internet than I did, resources in your native language, and a tech job market on your doorstep. You have every advantage I didn't have.

So how did it happen? That's what this chapter is about.

First, Let's Get One Thing Straight

You've seen the ads. "Quit your 9-to-5." "Six figures in 90 days with AI." Some guy leaning on a rented Lamborghini.

I'm not going to promise you any of that. Anyone who promises you specific money on a specific timeline is selling you a fantasy. And honestly, if this book were hype, this would be the part where I show you screenshots of my bank account. I won't, because that's not what this is.

Here's what I will promise: this book teaches a real, in-demand technical skill, in the actual order I wish someone had taught me. If you put in consistent hours, you will finish it having built working AI automations, not watched someone else build them. What you earn from that skill depends on what you do with it. My job is to make sure the skill is real. That part, I can guarantee.

Okay. Now the story.

The Unglamorous Story

I graduated high school in 2015 from the social studies track. Went on to study asset management in business school. After graduating in 2019, I got a job as an accounting assistant. Right on script: a degree, an office job, an entry-level salary, nine-to-five. Nothing wrong with any of it.

Then 2020 happened. COVID hit the company I worked for hard. Things got more uncertain by the week, and eventually I made a call: instead of waiting to be laid off, I quit and tried to build something of my own.

I took my savings and started a small business. I spent two full years on it. Two years of grinding, trial and error, and hope slowly running out. In the end, the business failed.

I still remember the week I finally admitted it was over. Checking my account balance and doing the math on how many months of food I had left. Avoiding calls from people who'd ask how the business was going. At an age when I was "supposed" to have my life together, I had no job, no savings, and no plan.

That was my lowest point. And it's exactly where I made the decision that changed everything.

In early 2022, I started learning to code from free YouTube tutorials. Complete beginner. No IT background, no friends in tech to ask, no money for a bootcamp. Just me, a cheap secondhand laptop, and the internet. But I had one thing going for me: I had nothing left to lose. So I went all in.

I was consistent. Studied every day, practiced every day. And in September 2022, less than nine months after writing my first line of code, I got hired as a Frontend Engineer. A regular office job. I did that for three years.

During those three years as a developer, I kept noticing the same thing over and over: so many processes at every company are still painfully manual. Copy-pasting data between tools. Sending emails one by one. Building reports by hand. I got curious: how do you automate all of that? And that's how I found AI Automation.

In December 2024, I started learning it seriously. I discovered a tool called n8n (pronounced "n-eight-n"), a platform that connects your apps together and makes entire processes run automatically, with AI doing the thinking in the middle. At first I understood none of it. But I was hooked. I learned from documentation, from trial and error, from experimenting every night after work.

I built small projects. Tiny automations. Posted them on LinkedIn. Picked up a few freelance clients. But when I tried to land a full-time role in AI Automation? Brutal.

That was my second lowest point. Working my full-time developer job from morning to afternoon. Studying automation and doing freelance work from evening until midnight. Sending applications to dozens of companies. Making it to final interviews, and then getting the rejection email. Again and again. There was a stretch where I genuinely thought about quitting: maybe this level just isn't for someone like me.

But I didn't stop. Kept learning, kept building, kept applying. And then one night in November 2025, an email came in that I read three times before I let myself believe it: an offer. Full-time, remote, AI Automation Engineer at an international company. They had candidates from everywhere. They picked the business major from Indonesia.

From the day I started learning AI Automation to the day I got that offer: less than one year.

Read that journey back: social studies kid, business degree, failed business owner, self-taught coder, three years in an office, studying nights and weekends, rejected over and over. And now, an AI Automation Engineer. If I could do it with every disadvantage stacked against me, you can absolutely do it.

What Does This Work Actually Look Like?

"AI Automation" sounds abstract, so let me make it concrete with a real example from my own work.

A company receives property documents all day long. Before automation, a person had to open each document, figure out what type it was, label it, check whether it was a duplicate, and file it in the right folder. Fifteen to twenty minutes per document. Thirty documents a day. That's one entire workday, every day, spent sorting files.

I built a workflow (an automated step-by-step process) where a document comes in, AI reads it, labels it, checks for duplicates, and files it. In seconds, untouched by human hands. One workday of labor, reduced to nothing, permanently.

That's the job: you find a slow, repetitive, expensive process, and you make it disappear. Businesses don't pay for this because it's trendy. They pay because it saves them real money every single month. And here's what the market pays for people who can do it: on Upwork, AI automation freelancers were averaging $85–120 per hour in early 2026, with independent consultants charging $150 an hour and up. A single workflow build, the kind you'll be capable of after finishing this book and practicing, typically sells for $200 to $1,500.

You'll build a simpler version of exactly this kind of system in Chapter 7. Not read about it. Build it.

Why AI Automation?

You've heard about AI. You've probably used ChatGPT. But for a lot of people, it all still feels like someone else's world. Something for engineers, for Silicon Valley, for people with the "right" degree.

Here's what makes this specific skill different:

1. Demand is exploding faster than supply. Every company is under pressure to adopt AI, but almost none of them have people who can actually connect AI to their real business processes. The number of businesses that need automation is growing far faster than the number of people who can build it. That gap is your opportunity, and it's open right now.

2. You don't need a CS degree. Your work experience is actually an asset. I'm living proof. Tools like n8n are visual: you build automations by connecting blocks on a screen, not by writing hundreds of lines of code. What you actually need is logic and an understanding of how businesses work. If you've ever worked in accounting, admin, sales, HR, teaching, retail, then you already understand business processes better than most engineers do. You know where the painful, repetitive work hides, because you've done it. That's your unfair advantage, not your weakness.

3. It's remote-native work. Everything runs in the cloud. You can do this job from anywhere with an internet connection, and companies hiring for these roles are already used to remote workers. No relocation required.

4. You can start earning before you're "job ready." This isn't a skill where you study for two years and then finally look for work. Every automation you learn to build in this book is something a real business would pay for. Freelance projects, consulting, selling templates: the monetization paths open up early (Chapter 8 covers exactly how).

And one more thing worth saying out loud: people in the US regularly pay $15,000–$20,000 for coding bootcamps to break into tech. The path in this book costs you a laptop you already own and tools with free tiers. The barrier isn't money. It's consistency.
